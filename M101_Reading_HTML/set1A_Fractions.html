Fractions

Working with fractions is a very important foundation to algebra. Here we will briefly review reducing, multiplying, dividing, adding, and subtracting fractions. As this is a review, concepts will not be explained in detail as other lessons are.

A fraction can be used to represent parts of a whole. The fraction bar shown above represents the fraction \(\frac{1}{3}\) since one of three equal parts of the bar is shaded. Recall that the integer \(7\) can be written as the fraction.

We always like our final answers when working with fractions to be reduced (the numerator and denominator are divided by their greatest common factor (GCF)). This is shown in the following example:

Example 1A-1: Reduce a Fraction

\(\dfrac{36}{84}\) Both numerator and denominator are divisible by \(4\)
\(\dfrac{36 \div 4}{84 \div 4} = \dfrac{9}{21}\) Both numerator and denominator are still divisible by \(3\)
\(\dfrac{9 \div 3}{21 \div 3} = \dfrac{3}{7}\) Our solution

The previous example could have been done in one step by dividing both numerator and denominator by \(12.\) We also could have divided by \(2\) twice and then divided by \(3\) once (in any order). It is not important which method we use as long as we continue reducing our fraction until it cannot be reduced any further. Another way to reduce th fraction in Example 1A-1 is given in the next example

Example 1A-1 Version 2: Reduce a Fraction

\(\dfrac{36}{84}\) Both numerator and denominator are divisible by \(12\)
\(36 = (3)(12)\) Write \(36\) as a product involving \(12\)
\(84 = (7)(12)\) Write \(84\) as a product involving \(12\)
\(\dfrac{36}{84}=\dfrac{(3)(12)}{ (7)(12)} \) Rewrite our fraction, \(\dfrac{12}{12}=1 \) (\(12\) parts of \(12\) is one whole)
\(\dfrac{(3)}{ (7)}\cdot\dfrac{(12)}{ (12)} = \dfrac{3}{7}\) Our solution

The last step above utilizes multiplication of fractions which is discussed next.

Multiplication and Division of Fractions

The easiest operation with fractions is multiplication. We can multiply fractions by multiplying straight across, multiplying numerators together and denominators together.

Example 1A-2: Multiply Fractions

\(\dfrac{6}{7} \cdot \dfrac{3}{5}\) Multiply numerators across and denominators across
\(\dfrac{18}{35}\) Our solution

When multiplying we can reduce our fractions before we multiply. We can either reduce vertically with a single fraction, or diagonally with several fractions, as long as we use one number from the numerator and one number from the denominator.

Example 1A-3: Multiply an Integer and a Fraction

\( (2)\left(\dfrac{2}{9}\right) \) Rewrite the integer \(2\) as a fraction
\( \left(\dfrac{2}{1}\right) \left(\dfrac{2}{9}\right) \) Multiply numerators across and denominators across
\( \dfrac{4}{9} \) Our solution

Example 1A-4: Multiply and Reduce

\(\dfrac{25}{24} \cdot \dfrac{32}{55}\) Reduce \(25\) and \(55\) by dividing by \(5\). Reduce \(32\) and \(24\) by dividing by \(8\)
\(\dfrac{5}{3} \cdot \dfrac{4}{11}\) Multiply numerators across and denominators across
\(\dfrac{20}{33}\) Our solution

It is often easier to write fractions in factored form, and cancel common factors BEFORE multiplying. Let's try Example 1A-04 again.

Example 1A-4 Version 2: Multiply and Reduce

\(\dfrac{25}{24} \cdot \dfrac{32}{55}\) Rewrite each fraction in factored form, as in Example 1A-1 Version 2
\(\dfrac{(5)(5)}{(3)(8)} \cdot \dfrac{(4)(8)}{(5)(11)}\) Write as one fraction, reorder the integers, collecting equal factors together
\(\dfrac{(5)(8)(5)(4)}{(5)(8)(3)(11)}\) \(\dfrac{5}{5}=1 \) and \(\dfrac{8}{8}=1 \) , cancel those factors
\(\dfrac{(5)(4)}{(3)(11)}\) Multiply numerators across and denominators across
\(\dfrac{20}{33}\) Our solution

Dividing fractions is very similar to multiplying with one extra step. Dividing fractions requires us to first take the reciprocal of the second fraction and multiply. Once we do this, the multiplication problem solves just as the previous problem.

Example 1A-5: Divide Fractions

\(\dfrac{21}{16} \div \dfrac{28}{6}\) Multiply by the reciprocal
\(\dfrac{21}{16} \cdot \dfrac{6}{28}\) Factor: \(21=(3)(7)\), \(16=(2)(8)\), \(6=(2)(3)\) and \(28=(4)(7)\)
\(\dfrac{(3)(7)}{(2)(8)} \cdot \dfrac{(2)(3)}{(4)(7)}\) Cancel: \(\dfrac{2}{2}=1\) and \(\dfrac{7}{7}=1\)
\(\dfrac{(3)}{(8)} \cdot \dfrac{(3)}{(4)}\) Multiply remaining numerators across and denominators across
\(\dfrac{9}{32}\) Our solution

Common Denominators

To add and subtract fractions we will first have to determine the least common denominator (LCD). There are several ways to determine an LCD. One way is to determine the smallest multiple of the larger denominator that is also divisible by the smaller denominator.

Example 1A-6: LCD

Determine the LCD of \(8\) and \(12\) Test multiples of \(12\)
\(12?\phantom{1234567}\dfrac{12}{8}\) No! Can't divide \(12\) by \(8\)
\(24?\phantom{123}\dfrac{24}{8}=3\) Yes! We can divide \(24\) by \(8\)
\(24\) Our solution

Addition and Subtraction of Fractions

Adding and subtracting fractions is identical in process. If both fractions already have a common denominator we just add or subtract the numerators and keep the denominator.

Example 1A-7: Add Fractions

\(\dfrac{7}{8}+\dfrac{3}{8}\) Same denominator, add numerators \(7+3\)
\(\dfrac{10}{8}\) Reduce answer, dividing by 2
\(\dfrac{5}{4}\) Our solution

While \(\dfrac{5}{4}\) can be written as the mixed number \(1\dfrac{1}{4}\) , in algebra we will almost never use mixed numbers. For this reason we will always use the improper fraction (the numerator is larger than the denominator), not the mixed number.

Example 1A-8: Subtract Fractions

\(\dfrac{13}{6}-\dfrac{9}{6}\) Same denominator, subtract numerators \(13-9\)
\(\dfrac{4}{6}\) Reduce answer, dividing by \(2\)
\(\dfrac{2}{3}\) Our solution

If the denominators do not match we will first have to identify the LCD and build up each fraction by multiplying the numerators and denominators by the same number so the denominator is built up to the LCD.

Example 1A-9: Add Fractions

\(\dfrac{5}{6}+\dfrac{4}{9}\) LCD is \(18\)
\(\dfrac{3\cdot 5}{3\cdot 6}+\dfrac{4\cdot 2}{9\cdot 2}\) Multiply first fraction by \(1=\dfrac{3}{3}\) and the second by \(1=\dfrac{2}{2}\)
\(\dfrac{15}{18}+\dfrac{8}{18}\) Same denominator, add numerators, \(15+8\)
\(\dfrac{23}{18}\) Our solution

Example 1A-10: Subtract Fractions

\(\dfrac{2}{3}-\dfrac{1}{6}\) LCD is \(6\)
\(\dfrac{2\cdot 2}{2\cdot 3}-\dfrac{1}{6}\) Multiply first fraction by \(1=\dfrac{2}{2}\), the second already has a denominator of \(6\)
\(\dfrac{4}{6}-\dfrac{1}{6}\) Same denominator, subtract numerators, \(4-1\)
\(\dfrac{3}{6}\) Reduce answer, dividing numerator and denominator by \(3\)
\(\dfrac{1}{2}\) Our solution
